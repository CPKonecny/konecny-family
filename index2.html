Executive Summary #

This site follows one American family across four centuries—from Pilgrim passengers John Howland & Elizabeth Tilley, to original proprietors in Massachusetts’ Swift River towns, to New England Planters in Nova Scotia, and Bohemian (Czech) immigrants who built new lives in Wisconsin—culminating in the present‑day Konecny line.

Chapter 5 · The New England Planters (Nova Scotia)

Patience Wood (sister to the direct line) marries Simeon Freeman; c.1761 they join ~8,000 Planters resettling Nova Scotia after the Acadian expulsion—founding communities like Liverpool.

Chapter 6 · Quabbin Valley Roots

Family proprietors help settle Swift River towns later lost to Boston’s Quabbin Reservoir (1930s). Four towns—Dana, Enfield, Greenwich, Prescott—were disincorporated; residents and cemeteries were relocated.

Part V — Union of Colonial & Immigrant Lines (1947) #

Chapter 8 · A 1947 Union: Marjorie Wood & Frank Konecny

Post‑WWII Wisconsin. Marjorie (journalism, UW–Madison) and Frank (U.S. Navy, Pacific) marry in 1947—symbolically joining colonial New England heritage with first‑generation immigrant grit.

  • Frank: decades at a tent & awning company → general manager.
  • Marjorie: society editor & award‑winning journalist; community leader.
Chapter 9 · Merging Legacies: Life as a Wood–Konecny Family
